Invariant sums of perfect powers $\DeclareMathOperator{\sgn}{sgn}$

We are interested in finding nontrivial sums of perfect powers that are exponentiation invariant. This means that if we swap the bases with the exponents, the sum remains the same.

Strongly Power Invariant Numbers (SPINs): Natural numbers $n\in\mathbb N$ equal to an exponentiation invariant sum of unique non-invariant perfect powers $a^b, a\gt 1, b\gt 1$. That is, for some $k\gt 1$ we can write: $$ n = \sum_{i=1}^k a_i^{b_i} = \sum_{i=1}^k b_i^{a_i}, \quad a_i\gt 1, b_i\gt 1, \quad a_i^{b_i} \ne b_i^{a_i}, \quad (i\ne j\implies\{a_i,b_i\}\ne\{a_j,b_j\}). $$ Then, $n$ is a $k$-order SPIN and we write $n\in G_k$.

Classification: To classify these numbers more nicely, we split $G_k$ into either union of $2^{k-1}$ disjoint subsets $$G_k=G^{(0)}_k \cup G^{(1)}_k \cup \dots \cup G^{(2^{k-1}-1)}_k = G^{(2^{k}-1)}_k \cup G^{(2^{k}-2)}_k \cup \dots \cup G^{(2^{k-1})}_k, $$ where $b$ in $G^{(b)}_k$ represents the binary evaluation of vector $v_n=(\sgn(a_i-b_i),i=1,\dots,k)$, for $\sgn(x)=\begin{cases}0 & x\lt 0\\1&x\gt 0\end{cases}$, under the assumption that $(i\lt j \implies \max\{a_i^{b_i}-b_i^{a_i},b_i^{a_i}-a_i^{b_i}\} \lt \max\{a_j^{b_j}-b_j^{a_j},b_j^{a_j}-a_j^{b_j}\} )$.
We write $n\in G^{(b)}_k$ and say that $n$ is a $k$-order $b$-genus SPIN.

Order $k\lt 5$ examples

Conjecture: If $k\lt 5$, then $G_k=\emptyset$. That is, there are no SPINs of order less than $5$.

If a (counter) example exists, it is larger than $n\gt 10^{20}$. Only three "almost" (counter) examples are known: $$\begin{array}{} 1546 &=& 2^7 + 8^2 + 5^4 + 3^6 \color{red}{-\frac12} &=& 7^2 + 2^8 + 4^5 + 6^3 \color{red}{+\frac12}\\ 3119 &=& 2^9 + 6^3 + 7^3 + 2^{11} \color{red}{-\frac12} &=& 9^2 + 3^6 + 3^7 + 11^2 \color{red}{+\frac12}\\ 16113 &=& 3^4 + 8^2 + 6^5 + 2^{13} \color{red}{+\frac12} &=& 4^3 + 2^8 + 5^6 + 13^2 \color{red}{-\frac12} \end{array}$$ If it weren't for the total error of $\color{red}{\pm 1}$, they would belong to $G^{(6)}_4$, because their $v_n=(0,1,1,0)$ if you look at the LHS. Alternatively, if you look at the RHS you can also say that they would belong to $G^{(9)}_4$, because there $v_n=(1,0,0,1)$.

WLOG, from now on we can observe only the classification relative to the LHS, as it is symmetric to the RHS one.

Order $k=5$ examples

Up to $n\le 10^{20}$, only $14$ examples or $5$-order SPINS are known. It is conjectured that these are the only examples.

Up to this limit, the largest known "near" example (the LHS and RHS differ by $\color{red}{\pm 1}$) is: $$\begin{array}{} {8590001473}&=& 2^{8}+2^{16}+4^{16}+2^{32}+33^{2}\color{red}{-\frac12}&=& 8^{2}+16^{2}+16^{4}+32^{2}+2^{33}\color{red}{+\frac12} \end{array}$$ If it weren't for the error of $\color{red}{\pm 1}$, it would be a $G^{(1)}_5$ example.

Notice that "near" examples of $k$-order correspond to examples of $(k+1)$-order that contain $\{a_i,b_i\}=\{2,3\}$. This is because $2^3-3^2=-1$ and $3^2-2^3=1$, so we can cancel out the error by adding this term to the sum.

Instead of keeping track of $k$-order "near" examples, we can instead look at $(k+1)$-order examples with term $\{2,3\}$.
